Design Patterns at DWP

Elements

Elements are the smallest standardised parts of design and include:

  • what fonts we use where
  • colours we can use
  • our headers and footers
  • banners
  • what buttons, checkboxes and other interactions look like

They generally will change rarely and usually centrally from Government Digital Service.

The vast majority of elements we use are GOV.UK elements but there are a few unique DWP elements.

See the DWP elements.

Pages and components

When elements are combined to present content designed for a distinct purpose they become components. These could be whole pages of a transaction, or parts of pages.

Examples would be how we:

  • tell someone they've made a mistake
  • warn someone they need to do something
  • let people create a list of things
  • show legal information
  • let people enter an address

The component or page pattern not only covers the standard presentation but also how common content should be worded.

Our component and page patterns build on GOV.UK patterns and are validated through user research.

They may change if there are updates to things such as the GOV.UK style guide.

See the DWP component design patterns

Interactions

Ineractions combine components and pages to give a discrete user experience. One way to test if something is an interaction is to see if it meets a whole user story or need in itself.

An example would be: "as a user I need to give my correct address so that I'll receive the things I need from DWP". This could include all the following components:

  • enter your own, valid postcode into a look-up
  • validate a postcode and warn of any errors
  • select from a pre-populated list (of addresses)
  • change an address manually

Interactions are often the focus of user research 'topic guides'. They may change relatively frequently as iterations of components and content gradually improve the user experience.

See the DWP Interaction Design Patterns

Services

Service patterns are transactions between DWP and its users that could apply to more than one service across the DWP portfolio.

A service pattern could be operational (involving DWP staff) or technical, or have elements of both. Digital interactions (or a group of them) will usually make up part of the service pattern, but a service pattern also includes what happens before and after user interactions.

Examples could be:

  • the help we offer to assisted digital customers
  • how user data is received and stored by DWP
  • communicating with people by email or text message
  • sending, storing and using customer documents
  • getting customers' consent to contact third parties

We'll be establishing service patterns as more DWP services go live and we learn more about what's needed.

As well as iterating through user research, analysis and feedback, service patterns could change if legislation changes.
